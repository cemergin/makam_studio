# TL;DR

- **The trade is small and personal.** Probably fewer than thirty active full-time qanun luthiers exist worldwide. Most are family shops; most are in Istanbul or Izmir. A handful work in diaspora (Berlin, Paris, Beirut, Istanbul-via-Aleppo). The Baghdad lineage is essentially extinct.
- **A serious instrument is expensive.** Tourist qanuns start at $300 and are usually unplayable; a student-grade handmade Turkish qanun runs $1,300–$2,500; professional instruments by Mustafa Sağlam, Kenan Özten, or the Eken/Güleç generation run $4,000–$15,000; ultra-microtonal commissions (Yarman 79-tone, Weiss Al-Kindî) run past $20,000.
- **Mandals are the heart of the engineering.** Brass-and-alpaca-silver levers paired with hardwood (boxwood, ebony) or bone fingertips, set against precisely placed pins, raise pitch by a calibrated comma. They are the part most likely to fail and the part players have re-set every few years by the maker.
- **The mandal click is part of the sound.** Players and makers describe the mechanical clack as punctuation, not noise. Any digital qanun should let users choose: subtle Turkish click, brighter Arab click, both, or off — not strip it out by default.
- **The Syrian war scattered a lineage.** Damascus and Aleppo housed living shops (the broader Nahat Christian-luthier tradition, makers like Nabil Qassis in Aleppo, the workshops that supplied Julien Weiss's Al-Kindî qanun) that are now reconstituted in diaspora or simply lost. makam_studio's support of Levantine, Egyptian, and Iraqi traditions is partly a digital archive of a fragile shop culture.
- **Free in the browser is a public good.** When the cheapest serious instrument costs more than a month's rent in most of the maqam world's home cities, a working digital qanun is genuinely democratising — that framing belongs on the about page, not in a footnote.

# 1. Major active makers and their lineages

The qanun is not built in factories. With the partial exception of Egyptian and Chinese tourist-trade instruments, every professional qanun in current circulation came out of a one-person or family workshop. The list below is incomplete on purpose — many makers do not have a web presence, and several of the most respected names work mostly through word of mouth among players. Where romanizations differ I have included alternates.

## 1.1 Turkish (Istanbul, Izmir, Ankara)

**Ali Eken (İstanbul, ca. 1960s–present).** Eken is the Istanbul maker most often named when contemporary players are asked who built their instruments. The major reference point is **Göksel Baktagir** (b. 1966), who has performed with an Eken-built qanun for most of his recording career and is the most internationally visible Turkish qanun virtuoso. Eken's signature instruments use seasoned walnut bodies, spruce or cedar tops, hardwood pegboxes, and brass-and-alpaca-silver mandals. Build time for a top-tier Eken instrument is reported by players as roughly six months from raw stock, on top of years of wood seasoning. (Note: web search returns a cluster of unrelated Turkish public figures named "Ali Eken" or "Ali Nihat Eken"; the luthier maintains a low online footprint and is most reliably reached through Istanbul-based players.)

**Ejder Güleç (İzmir, 1939–2014).** The "premiere kanun maker" of the modern Turkish era according to Armenian-American musician Ara Dinkjian, who wrote a tribute on his death. Güleç's reputation: "for the longest time if you wanted the best, it was an Ejder kanun." He was the maker who collaborated with **Ozan Yarman** on the 79-tone qanun in 2005, fitting mandals to Yarman's specifications, and he also built **prototypes for Julien Jalâl Eddine Weiss**'s Al-Kindî microtonal designs. His shop traded as "Ejder Güleç & Sons." His son(s) and several apprentices continue the lineage; Erdal Çetin, Şahin Sert, and others learned mandal craft in proximity to him.

**Kenan Özten (İstanbul / Turkey).** A widely-cited maker whose strings (PVF and DuPont sets, 78-string complete sets) are sold globally. Özten's shop also produces full instruments and is regarded by retailers as among the best in Turkey. Özten is one of the names most consistently associated with PVF string production — his sets are the de-facto Turkish standard for non-gut instruments.

**Mustafa Sağlam (Ankara).** Probably the most globally distributed contemporary Turkish qanun maker via online retailers (Sala Muzik, Ethnic Musical, Reverb, Etsy). His instruments use ebony tuning pegs, oak pegboxes, alpaca-silver mandals, PVF strings, and 26 three-string courses (78 strings total). Saglam's workshop sits at a price point ($1,300–$2,500 retail) that makes him the "if you actually plan to play seriously, start here" maker for international students.

**Erdal Çetin (Karşıyaka, İzmir, b. 1961).** Not a full-instrument luthier per se but the dominant **mandal and parts supplier** in Turkey. Trained at İzmir Çınarlı Industrial School (mechanical, 1978), apprenticed in moulding workshops, then collaborated with Ejder Güleç and Şahin Sert. In 1992 he industrialised mandal production using alpacca silver and modern turning methods. His client list — published on his own site — includes essentially every active Turkish maker: Ejder GÜLEÇ, Ömer AKPINAR, Kenan ÖZTEN, Muzaffer OKUMUŞ, Erkin GÜRDOĞAN, Şahin SERT, Sadettin SANDI, Mustafa SAĞLAM, İbrahim AVCI, Sinan ŞAHİN, Ümit BOLU, Taner KARAAĞAÇLI, İbrahim BAKIM, Cengiz SARIKUŞ (also a noted oud maker), Okan EROL, Tacettin ŞEHRİ, Hüsamettin SAYACIOĞLU, İsmail EDİZ, Ali DONAT, Erhan PUYAN, Metin KESİCİ, Salim ÖZER, Serkan ÇELİK, Ahmet ÇEŞME, Atilla SARP, plus international diaspora makers Mustafa Bilici (Germany), Niko Magdalinos (Greece), Tuncay Kahraman (France), İxtiyar Seyidov (Azerbaijan), and Yahya Aliyari (Iran). This list is the closest thing to a current census of the trade.

**The earlier Turkish line.** Before Eken/Güleç, Istanbul's qanun-making was largely Armenian and Greek-speaking, embedded in the polyphonic luthier culture of pre-WWI Pera and Galata. The standard historical reference is **Mahmut Usta**, an Istanbul maker credited (around 1876, per Sala Muzik's history page) with the more recent type of qanun that scholars distinguish from older models. Earlier still, **Ömer Efendi** of Damascus is widely cited as the musician who introduced the modernized lever-bearing qanun to the Ottoman court during the reign of Mahmud II (1818–1839). Names attached to the Müzika-yı Hümâyun and the Mevlevi tekkes are documented in archival ensemble rosters more than in luthier rolls; the imperial workshops appear not to have produced standardized qanun makers in the way they did with violins or pianos. Names like Saraboğlu, Aktulga, and Hayri Tümer that circulate in player oral tradition could not be reliably anchored to instrument-making rather than playing in the available sources, and should be treated as oral attribution pending interview confirmation.

## 1.2 Syrian (Damascus and Aleppo, mostly diaspora)

**Nabil Qassis / Kassis (Aleppo).** The single best-documented contemporary Syrian qanun maker. He built **Maya Youssef**'s qanun before her 2007 emigration to the UK; the instrument is white maple with crystal/synthetic strings and chrome-plated levers ("so they never rust"). Qassis is the Aleppine analogue to Eken in Istanbul: the maker players sought when commissioning a serious instrument.

**The Nahat lineage (Damascus).** The Nahat family — Greek (Rûm) Orthodox Christians who came to Damascus from Mardin — are primarily an oud-making dynasty, but the broader Damascus shop tradition they anchored extended to qanun. The lineage runs Abdo Nahat (founder, late 19th century) → Hanna, George, Toufik, and others; surviving museum-grade instruments are dated 1897 onward. Their cultural role is the pivotal one: the proof that a single Christian craft family could sustain four generations of world-class oriental-instrument making in Damascus. The 2011 war broke that continuity. SyriacPress's June 2025 essay framed them as "the last oudmakers of Daramsuq."

**Aleppo's Sufi-music infrastructure.** Aleppo was the centre of *tarab* (urban Arab art-music) practice and the home of the workshops that supplied Julien Weiss's diwan halabi. Many of these workshops are gone, in pieces, or in diaspora. **Yaser Hudair** (also spelled Yasser Khudair) — a Syrian oud and qanun performer profiled in the Migration of Art (Sanatın Göçü) project — works from a Topkapı, Istanbul workshop; his story is representative of the displacement.

## 1.3 Lebanese

The Beirut shop tradition exists but is poorly documented online. Pierre Maakaron and Alfred Chebat are cited in player oral tradition as Beirut-based makers but no useful biographical data surfaces from open web sources; the Khalifeh family operates a long-running Beirut instrument shop primarily known for ouds. Several Lebanese qanun players in the past three decades have commissioned from Aleppo or Damascus rather than locally — a sign that Beirut's shop ecology, while real, runs smaller than its Syrian neighbour's.

## 1.4 Egyptian

**Mohamed Abdo Saleh (1912–?, Cairo)** is documented as a player-designer rather than a pure luthier: born in Cairo to a musician family, started qanun at twelve, replaced Ibrahim al-Aryan as Umm Kulthum's qanun player in 1929, and influenced Egyptian qanun design and stringing conventions through that role. The Egyptian school favours nylon over PVF, four mandals per course (instead of seven on the Turkish), and a slightly larger soundbox with five skin insets supporting a five-pillar bridge — these are choices passed down through Cairo workshops more than through any single named maker. Online retailers list "Egyptian handmade" qanuns from $899 upward; named individual makers behind these instruments are not consistently surfaced in English-language sources.

## 1.5 Iraqi

The Baghdad qanun tradition is historically critical and currently scattered. **Abraham Salman / Avraham (Ibrahim) Salman (1931–2014)** — the "King of the Qanun" — was a product of the Baghdad School for the Blind for Jewish children and a principal qanun in the Royal Iraqi Radio Orchestra of the 1930s–'40s, when 247 of 250 orchestra members were Jewish. **Yusuf Za'arur (1902–?)** and **Yusuf Za'arur al-Kabir** (b. ca. 1867) anchored the older generation. Salman emigrated to Israel and continued playing into the 21st century. The instrument he played was a Baghdad-built qanun (provenance not pinned in available sources), and the workshops that produced such instruments did not survive the post-1948 demographic upheaval that emptied Baghdad of its Jewish musical class.

## 1.6 Tunisian / Algerian (Maghrebi)

The qanun is an essential instrument in Andalusian-school ensembles: **al-âla** (Morocco), **gharnati** (western Algeria), **malouf** (Tunisia). It appears in conservatory curricula in Tunis, Algiers, and Tlemcen. Named makers in these countries do not surface easily in English-language sources; Tunisian players such as Farah Fersi hold conservatory diplomas in qanun but do not name their instrument's builder publicly. The plausible reading: Maghrebi qanuns are mostly imported from Cairo and Istanbul, with local repair-shops handling maintenance.

## 1.7 Greek (kanonaki)

**The Tsakirian workshop (Athens).** The Tsakirian family is Armenian-Greek; Agop Tsakirian arrived in Greece as a refugee from Smyrna (Izmir) in 1922, and the workshop has run for four generations. Onnik (second generation) operated the Athens shop from 1960; Karolos (third generation) returned to Athens from Astoria, Queens in 1993; Tanya (fourth generation, female luthier) continues the line today. Tsakirian instruments are most famous for bouzouki but include kanonaki, lute, oud, and classical instruments. The shop is a cultural anchor for Greek and rebetiko music. Niko Magdalinos (also Greece-based, listed as a Çetin client) is another active kanonaki maker.

## 1.8 Custom microtonal builders

Two named projects deserve their own paragraph because they pushed the engineering of the instrument further than tradition normally allows:

**The Al-Kindî qanun (Julien Jalâl Eddine Weiss, 1953–2015).** Weiss commissioned a custom instrument from a Turkish maker — Ejder Güleç built prototypes; Weiss describes it in an Al Jadid interview as "custom-made for me by a Turkish maker but [with] the dimension of an Arabic qanun." Critically, "as many as 15 mandals for some strings" to capture "subtle pitch variations between different maqamat that notation alone cannot capture." Weiss's instrument is the canonical example of an Arabic-school-played, Turkish-engineered, microtonally-extreme qanun.

**The 79-tone qanun (Ozan Yarman, 2005).** Built in Izmir by Ejder Güleç & Sons to Yarman's specifications, implementing his 79 MOS 159-tET tuning. Each string carries up to 27 mandals (7 flats + 1 natural + 8 sharps + 12 to double-sharp, mapped through a colour-coded scheme with naturals in royal-fuchsia). Fine tuners — added on Uğur Keçecioğlu's suggestion — sit beyond the bridge for sub-mandal pitch trim. The instrument is the engineering limit case: it shows what is mechanically possible when you abandon the constraint of a tradition's notation system. Its mass-loading on the strings, bridge wear, and mechanism crowding are documented engineering challenges; this is not a daily-driver instrument.

# 2. Materials science

## 2.1 Soundbox

The frame of a Turkish qanun is typically **walnut** (*Juglans regia*), occasionally maple or beech. Walnut's combination of density, dimensional stability across humidity swings, and the speed at which it transmits flexural waves makes it a default. Arabic qanuns frequently use **mahogany** or other tropical hardwoods, partly for tonal warmth and partly because of historical supply lines from East Africa and the Levant.

The soundboard — the resonating top, distinct from the frame — is most often **spruce** (preferred Turkish: Bosnian or Carpathian spruce) or **cedar**. Some modern Egyptian instruments substitute lighter local woods. Wood is seasoned for **two to seven years** before being cut for an instrument; rushing seasoning is the most common cause of long-term tonal collapse.

## 2.2 Bridges and the "windows"

The qanun's defining acoustic feature is the bridge resting on **skin panels** — historically calf or fish skin — set into rectangular openings ("windows") cut in the soundboard. Turkish qanuns use **four** windows under a single bridge on four arching pillars; Arabic qanuns use **five**. The skin is part of the resonator; it stretches and relaxes with humidity, which is part of why the instrument's tone is famously "earthy yet angelic."

The contemporary debate among players: **traditional skin (calf, fish) vs. modern PVF (polyvinyl fluoride film, e.g. DuPont Tedlar®)**. Skin produces a darker, more nasal timbre — what older Egyptian and Damascus recordings sound like — but is unstable, vulnerable to humidity, and increasingly subject to CITES-related supply problems for traditional cuts of calf. PVF is brighter, more stable, easier to source, and now the default in serious new Turkish builds. Players split: Maya Youssef's Aleppo-built instrument uses synthetic strings and chrome levers; older Cairo-school instruments often retain skin.

## 2.3 Strings

A serious qanun uses **78 strings in 26 courses of three** (Turkish standard) or **72–81 strings in courses of three** (Arabic variation). Three strings per course gives volume, redundancy, and a chorus effect — a slight detuning across the three is a deliberate part of the sound, in the same way Cuban tres or twelve-string guitar choruses are.

Material splits by school:
- **Nylon** — Egyptian and many Arabic-school instruments. Warmer, slightly slower attack.
- **PVF (polyvinyl fluoride / fluorocarbon)** — Turkish-school standard since the late 20th century. Brighter, faster attack, better tuning stability. Kenan Özten's PVF sets are de facto reference.
- **Gut** — historic; some Damascus shops kept gut into the late 20th century. Now rare and expensive.
- **Wound metal/silver-on-nylon** — bass courses, both schools.

Wire diameters typically run 0.60–1.20 mm depending on register.

## 2.4 Plectra

Two regional traditions:

- **Tırnak** (Turkish): a metal-and-leather thimble worn on the index finger, producing the characteristic Turkish attack. Tortoise shell historically; now more commonly buffalo horn, plastic, or carbon fibre due to CITES.
- **Rīsha** (Arabic, "feather"): a flexible plectrum traditionally cut from cow horn, leather, or actual feathers, played held between the fingers rather than worn. Produces a softer, more legato sound.

Plectra wear out — a serious player replaces them every several months.

## 2.5 Mandals

Mandals are the most engineering-sensitive part of the instrument:

- **Lever body**: brass or **alpaca silver** (German silver, Cu-Ni-Zn). Alpaca is now the standard since Erdal Çetin's 1992 industrialisation of mandal production. Brightness of the metal alloy directly affects how the click sounds when the mandal lands.
- **Pivot pin and contact pin**: small brass pins set into the soundboard or a hardwood inset.
- **Fingertip / thumb-piece** (the player-facing top of the lever): historically **bone**, **ivory** (now restricted), or hardwood — **boxwood** (*Buxus sempervirens*) and **ebony** (*Diospyros*) are typical. These woods are chosen for hardness, low friction, and resistance to fingertip oils.

Number of mandals per course: Turkish qanuns have **5–7 mandals per course** (or up to 12 on advanced instruments); Arabic qanuns traditionally use **4 mandals per course**. The Yarman/Weiss class has up to 27.

# 3. The mandal mechanism in detail

## 3.1 Mechanical operation

A mandal is a small lever that pivots on a horizontal pin set into the soundboard near the pegbox. When the lever is rotated downward by the player's left thumb, a bone or hardwood foot at the lever's base presses the string against a tiny pin (the "stop pin") set into the soundboard. This shortens the vibrating length of the string by a calibrated amount, raising pitch by a precise comma (typically a Holdrian or Pythagorean comma fraction).

When released, the lever rotates back up under spring tension, and the string returns to its open length. A single course's three strings all share the same lever — pressing once retunes all three.

The player can engage multiple mandals on the same course simultaneously, summing their pitch shifts. This is what makes a qanun a microtonal instrument rather than a quartertone instrument — the comma resolution is finer than quartertones, and the available pitches per course is the *cumulative* count of mandal combinations, not just the lever count.

## 3.2 Failure modes

A serious player has the mandals re-set every few years. Common failures:

- **Spring fatigue.** The lever no longer snaps back fully; pitch drifts downward over a phrase. Fix: replace spring, possibly re-tension housing.
- **Wood wear at pivot.** The lever sticks or rocks loosely. Fix: hardwood inset replacement.
- **Brass corrosion.** Especially in humid climates (Lebanon, Egypt, Mediterranean coasts). Levers tarnish, then bind. Fix: clean and lubricate; in extreme cases re-plate.
- **Mis-spaced stop pins.** The most subtle failure: pin drift of even 0.1 mm shifts pitch by several cents, which a trained ear hears immediately. Fix: pull pin, reposition, re-test against a reference. This is the work that requires the maker's ear, not just hands.
- **Mass loading from too many mandals.** On Yarman/Weiss-class instruments, the total mass of the lever array damps the string, lowering sustain and changing timbre. There is no clean fix; it is a design trade-off.

## 3.3 The Yarman / Weiss "mandal explosion"

When you stack 15–27 mandals per course you confront a set of simultaneous engineering constraints: bridge wear from the increased downward force; mechanism crowding (each lever needs clearance to swing); player ergonomics (the left thumb has limits on how many distinct positions it can find quickly); and acoustic loading (mass on the string changes the spectrum). Both Yarman's instrument and Weiss's instrument were experimental answers to "how far can this go" — they are ancestors of any digital qanun that wants to expose every comma without compromise.

# 4. Shop practice

## 4.1 Build time

Build time figures cluster around **six months for a top-tier professional Turkish qanun** in active workshop time, on top of **two to seven years** of wood seasoning before construction begins. Mid-tier instruments compress this: a Sağlam-class workshop produces serious instruments faster (a few months) by holding pre-seasoned stock and using consistent jigs. Tourist instruments come together in days from kit-grade stock.

## 4.2 Apprenticeship and lineage

Most workshops run as family lineages:
- **Tsakirian (Athens)** — four generations, Armenian-Greek refugee origin, with Tanya as the first female luthier in the line.
- **Nahat (Damascus)** — four generations of Christian oud-and-instrument makers; lineage broken by 2011 war.
- **Eken / Güleç** — two-generation Turkish lineages; Eken's line continues, Güleç's ended with the master's 2014 death (sons continue the shop).
- **Sağlam, Özten, Çetin** — single-master operations with apprentices but not formalised dynastic transmission.

The apprenticeship transmits **tacit** knowledge: which billet of walnut to season; how to "voice" a top by selective sanding while listening; how to set the stop-pin spacing for a particular target tuning; how to feel for spring tension. None of this lives in books. When a master dies without an apprentice, the lineage's tuning ear dies with him.

## 4.3 Modern threats

- **Apprentice pipeline.** Young Turks, Lebanese, and Syrians who would historically have apprenticed are now likelier to choose university or emigration. Several active masters in Istanbul are over sixty.
- **Materials supply.** CITES restrictions on tortoise shell (plectra), traditional ivory (mandal tips), and certain rosewoods (pegs). Calf-skin supply for traditional bridges is uneven. Aleppo's wood supply network was disrupted by the war.
- **Cheap competition.** Tourist-grade Egyptian and Chinese qanuns flood entry markets in Cairo, Istanbul, and online. They are typically unplayable but they crowd out student instruments at the bottom of the price ladder.
- **War.** The Syrian war (2011–) displaced much of the Damascus and Aleppo luthier ecology; some makers reconstituted in Istanbul, Beirut, Berlin, or Paris, others lost their tools and stock and never restarted.

# 5. Pricing tier

The price ladder, calibrated as of mid-2020s, in USD:

- **Tourist instrument ($300–1,000).** Often labelled "Egyptian handmade" or "Turkish handmade" but produced in semi-industrial conditions. Skin or cheap PVF, tuning that drifts within hours, mandals that bind. Frequently unplayable for serious music; sometimes used as a wall ornament.
- **Student instrument ($1,500–4,000).** Mustafa Sağlam's lower-tier walnut models, comparable Egyptian shop instruments, second-hand Turkish trade instruments. Real PVF, alpaca-silver mandals, holds tuning, can be re-mandaled. Where a serious learner should start.
- **Professional instrument ($4,000–15,000+).** Top-tier Sağlam, Özten, Eken, post-2014 Güleç-lineage shop, Aleppo (where available), top Egyptian. Full materials story: aged walnut, hand-fitted mandals, custom mandal-count specs, signed soundboards. The tier most concert players use.
- **Custom microtonal ($20,000+).** Weiss/Yarman-class. Bespoke mandal counts, fine-tuners beyond the bridge, custom wood selection, multi-year wait. Usually one-of-one.
- **Used / collector market.** Pre-war Damascus instruments, older Eken or Güleç originals, Aktulga-attributed historic instruments — collector pricing varies wildly, can match or exceed top-end new builds.

This pricing matters for makam_studio. **A serious entry-level qanun costs more than two months of median household income in Cairo, Damascus, or Tunis.** A free, browser-based maqam instrument is therefore not just a convenience product; it is an infrastructure act for a music tradition whose physical instrument has priced itself out of casual access.

# 6. The "mandal click" — sound and aesthetics

When a mandal is engaged, it produces a small mechanical click — the sound of brass and bone striking string and pin. Players and makers consistently describe this not as noise but as **musical punctuation**. The Turkish click (small lever, alpaca silver, hardwood foot) is brighter, sharper, and shorter; the Arabic click (slightly larger lever, often softer woods, occasional skin under the foot) is duller and rounder. They are different acoustic signatures, and listeners trained on the tradition recognise them.

The composer-performer Bukrek Erdoğan's framing — that the click is "punctuation" — is the dominant view. Recordings by Göksel Baktagir or Maya Youssef foreground the click; in fact, when emulator software strips it, the result sounds uncanny to ears familiar with the live instrument.

**Implication for makam_studio.** The click should be **on by default**, with a per-tradition acoustic signature (Turkish vs. Arab profile), and a user-controllable spectrum: subtle, full, both visual and audio, audio only, visual only, or off. The default should not be "off" — that would erase part of what the qanun *is*. The dropdown sequence we propose:
- *Subtle* (default): low-volume Turkish-profile click on mandal engage and release.
- *Full*: audible click with reverberant tail and slight pitch component.
- *Visual + audio*: both haptic UI feedback and audio.
- *Visual only*: animated mandal lever, no audio.
- *Off*: pure pitch-shift, no acoustic or visual mandal feedback.

# 7. Implications for makam_studio

The luthier research changes how makam_studio should position itself in three concrete ways.

**A. Lead the about page with the economic-access framing.** A serious qanun is $1,500 minimum, $4,000–$15,000 for the instrument a young Damascus or Cairo student would actually want. A free, working browser instrument is a public good in a precise, defensible sense — the same sense in which open-source software is. This is a stronger pitch than "we love maqam music" because it grounds the political claim in price data the user can verify in five minutes of looking at Sala Muzik or Ethnic Musical's storefronts.

**B. Mandal click is a feature, not noise.** Default to *subtle*, expose all five options, and model two acoustic profiles (Turkish, Arab) tied to the user's selected tradition. The decision to model the click is the decision to take the instrument seriously rather than to treat it as a "MIDI keyboard with quartertones."

**C. Build a credits / about page that names active makers.** A future v1.x or v2 page should name and link Ali Eken, the Eken-lineage workshop, Ejder Güleç (in memoriam, 1939–2014), Kenan Özten, Mustafa Sağlam, Erdal Çetin (mandals), Nabil Qassis (Aleppo), the Tsakirian workshop (Athens), the Nahat lineage (Damascus, in memoriam of the pre-2011 shop ecology), and the Yarman/Weiss microtonal collaboration. This should be done in consultation with players (Baktagir, Karaduman, Pinarbaşı, Youssef) wherever possible — the names matter and the makers should approve being listed.

**D. Future collaboration vector: signature qanun presets.** A v2 idea worth exploring: invite makers (or their estates) to commission a digital qanun preset that mirrors a real-world signature instrument — "Eken/Baktagir signature qanun," "Yarman 79-tone preset," "Aleppo school (Qassis tribute)." This is a way to drive attention to active makers from a free-instrument userbase. Done with the makers' explicit consent and credit, it could be a small but real form of giving back.

**E. The Syrian-Iraqi diaspora thread is part of the project's why.** makam_studio's commitment to Levantine, Egyptian, and Iraqi maqam traditions is not just musicological completeness. It is digital archive work for repertoires whose physical instrument-making infrastructure is endangered or scattered. That framing belongs in the manifesto.

# Open questions

1. **Who exactly built Abraham Salman's qanun?** Iraqi maker provenance is not well documented in available English sources. Worth pursuing through Hebrew-language archives at Hebrew University's Jewish Music Research Centre.
2. **Does Ali Eken have a website or contact path other than direct introduction?** Web search returns nothing definitive. May require an Istanbul-based informant or direct Baktagir contact.
3. **What did the pre-1960s Saraboğlu / Aktulga / Hayri Tümer line actually build?** These names circulate in player oral tradition but did not anchor in available sources as luthiers (Tümer was a neyzen, not a kanun maker, per available evidence). Worth interview-confirming with senior Istanbul players.
4. **Cairo's contemporary qanun makers by name.** "Egyptian handmade" is a marketing label; named individuals behind these instruments are not surfaced in English-language sources. Likely accessible through Cairo Conservatoire or Bayt al-Oud network.
5. **Maghrebi qanun makers.** Tunisian and Algerian conservatory-trained players exist; their instruments' provenance is likely Cairo or Istanbul, but local Maghrebi makers may exist undocumented.
6. **The mandal click's exact spectrum — measured.** No available source provides waveform analysis of Turkish vs. Arab mandal clicks. A short field-recording project (with permission) could anchor the acoustic profile choices in measurement rather than impression.
7. **Surviving Damascus shops post-2024 ceasefire.** With the post-Assad reconstitution, are any pre-war Damascus luthiers returning? Worth tracking via the Atlantic Council's Syria reporting and SyriacPress.
